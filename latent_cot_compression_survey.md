# Latent Chain-of-Thought Representation Initialization & Broader CoT Compression: A Comparative Survey

This document expands Section 3.1 ("Representation Initialization") of _Reasoning Beyond Language: A Comprehensive Survey on Latent Chain-of-Thought Reasoning_ (Chen, Zhao, Xia et al., arXiv:2505.16782v2) into a full comparison table covering all 28 methods the survey cites (15 Hidden State, 5 Weighted Embedding, 8 Special Vector), then extends the picture with 10 additional CoT-compression papers researched separately. The **Compute Requirement** column (Low/Medium/High) is inferential, synthesised from clues like model scale, GPU counts, and training-stage descriptions reported in each paper; it is not a figure reported by any author and should be treated as an educated judgement rather than a sourced fact. Where a paper does not report a directly comparable number, the table uses qualitative language rather than inventing one.

| 1   | **Coconut** — Hao et al., 2024/2025, arXiv:2412.06769 (COLM 2025)                         | Feeds the LLM's last hidden state directly back as the next input embedding instead of decoding it to a token; trained via a multi-stage curriculum that progressively replaces language reasoning steps with "continuous thoughts," bracketed by `<bot>`/`<eot>` markers.                                                                                                                | On GPT-2 scale: ProsQA accuracy 97.0% vs. 77.5% for explicit CoT (large logical-planning gain); but GSM8K accuracy 34.1% vs. 42.9% for CoT. Uses ~8.2 forward passes vs. CoT's ~25 on GSM8K.                                                                                        | **Medium** — curriculum SFT on small (GPT‑2) models is cheap to train, but inference needs _N+1_ strictly sequential forward passes (one per latent thought, no KV‑cache parallelism across thoughts), so latency scales with chain length.                                                       | Genuine breadth‑first exploration of multiple reasoning paths in one latent vector; large gains on search/planning‑heavy tasks; far fewer forward passes than CoT.                                                                                                                                | Underperforms plain CoT on arithmetic (GSM8K); curriculum schedule is brittle and sensitive to the number of latent thoughts per step; sequential latent decoding caps throughput.                                   |
| 2   | **CODI** — Shen et al., 2025, arXiv:2502.21074 (EMNLP 2025)                               | A single model is simultaneously trained as teacher (explicit CoT) and student (implicit CoT) via self‑distillation, aligning the student's hidden state at the answer‑predicting token with the teacher's via an L1 loss; no curriculum needed.                                                                                                                                          | First implicit‑CoT method to **match explicit CoT accuracy on GSM8K** at GPT‑2 scale while achieving **3.1× token compression**, beating the prior best implicit method by 28.2 points; reaches up to 7.8–8.2× compression (5.9× wall‑clock speedup) on longer GSM8K‑Aug‑NL chains. | **Medium** — one model trained jointly as teacher+student (no second network), GPT‑2/LLaMA‑1B scale; modest training cost, cheap inference (≈8 latent tokens, no curriculum scheduling).                                                                                                          | Closes the implicit‑vs‑explicit accuracy gap for the first time; single‑model self‑distillation is simpler than teacher‑student setups; latents can be partially decoded for interpretability.                                                                                                    | Still GPT‑2/1B‑scale validated only; relies on a single distillation anchor token, which can act as a shortcut if not regularized; compression ratio is dataset‑dependent.                                           |
| 3   | **LatentSeek** — Li et al., 2025, arXiv:2505.13308                                        | Test‑time‑only: leaves model weights frozen and instead applies policy‑gradient updates directly to the latent representation at inference, guided by a self‑generated reward, then decodes and re‑evaluates iteratively (Test‑Time Instance‑level Adaptation).                                                                                                                           | Outperforms CoT prompting and fine‑tuning baselines on GSM8K, MATH‑500, AIME2024; e.g. +15.2 pts over Best‑of‑N on GSM8K, +18.1 over SimpleRL‑Zoo on GSM8K/MATH‑500 (LLaMA‑3.1‑8B); with an oracle verifier, a 1.5B model jumps from 54.8%→82.8% on MATH‑500.                       | **Medium** — no fine‑tuning at all (training‑free), but each query requires several rounds of policy‑gradient updates to its own latent vector at test time, shifting cost from training to per‑query inference.                                                                                  | No training required, so it layers on top of any existing model; benefits compound with more optimization iterations (a new test‑time scaling axis); strong gains even over RL‑tuned baselines.                                                                                                   | Per‑query optimization adds latency that grows with iteration count; final latents can become semantically unintelligible to humans even when correct, hurting interpretability/debuggability.                       |
| 4   | **System‑1.5 Reasoning** — Wang et al., 2025, arXiv:2505.18962 (NeurIPS 2025)             | Introduces two "dynamic shortcuts": a _model‑depth shortcut_ that early‑exits non‑critical tokens through lightweight adapters while critical tokens go through full depth, and a _language‑to‑latent_ horizontal shortcut; trained via two‑stage distillation from a CoT teacher.                                                                                                        | On GSM8K, matches CoT‑fine‑tuned accuracy while accelerating inference **>20×** and cutting token generation by **~91–92%** on average.                                                                                                                                             | **Low‑Medium** — two‑stage distillation training is parallelizable (avoids Coconut/iCoT‑style curriculum scheduling), and lightweight adapter branches keep the trained model's own inference cost low.                                                                                           | Combines vertical (depth) and horizontal (sequence) efficiency in one framework; very large measured speedup; avoids brittle curriculum training.                                                                                                                                                 | Two distinct shortcut mechanisms add architectural complexity; gains are demonstrated primarily on math (GSM8K); depends on a strong teacher model for the distillation stage.                                       |
| 5   | **PCCoT** — Wu et al., 2025, arXiv:2506.18582 (EMNLP 2025)                                | Replaces Coconut's _sequential_ latent‑thought decoding with **Jacobi iteration**: all latent thought tokens are updated in parallel across a fixed number of iterations instead of one at a time; subsumes Pause Tokens (1 iteration) and full Coconut (iterations = #thoughts) as special cases.                                                                                        | With a well‑chosen iteration count, matches or **exceeds** Coconut/CODI‑style baselines while cutting **training and inference time by ~50%**; reported as more stable/robust to train than sequential Coconut.                                                                     | **Low‑Medium** — explicitly engineered to roughly halve the training and inference compute of sequential latent‑CoT methods via parallel updates.                                                                                                                                                 | Directly attacks the main compute bottleneck of latent‑CoT (sequential decoding); more stable training than Coconut; backward‑compatible (recovers prior methods as special cases).                                                                                                               | Benefit depends on tuning the iteration count vs. thought count trade‑off; only evaluated on GPT‑2‑scale GSM8K‑style data so far; still needs LoRA/SFT, not training‑free.                                           |
| 6   | **KaVa** — Kuzina et al., 2025, arXiv:2510.02312                                          | Distills the teacher's **compressed KV‑cache** (not just a single hidden state) into the student's latent trajectory via self‑distillation, building on PCCoT's parallel decoding (T=3 iterations); provides a dense, step‑wise supervisory signal instead of one anchor token.                                                                                                           | Consistently **outperforms CODI/PCCoT‑style baselines**, with a notably smaller accuracy drop moving from synthetic GSM8K‑Aug to more realistic GSM8K‑Aug‑NL data, indicating better scalability to natural‑language CoT.                                                           | **Medium‑High** — requires computing/distilling a _compressed_ KV‑cache trajectory from a teacher (not just one hidden state), a more complex training pipeline than single‑token distillation methods like CODI.                                                                                 | Step‑wise KV‑cache supervision is richer than endpoint‑only supervision; degrades more gracefully on harder, longer natural‑language CoT than prior methods.                                                                                                                                      | Added KV‑cache‑distillation machinery increases training complexity; builds on/depends on PCCoT's parallel decoding scheme rather than being a standalone simple recipe.                                             |
| 7   | **FR‑Ponder** — He & Tang, 2025, arXiv:2509.24238                                         | Keeps the LLM backbone completely **frozen**; a <1M‑parameter controller observes hidden states and decides, per instance, whether to halt or apply an extra "ponder" step by adding a pre‑computed steering vector to the representation, trained with GRPO.                                                                                                                             | Reported to deliver instance‑adaptive reasoning‑depth allocation that improves accuracy/compute trade‑offs over uniform‑depth baselines (Best‑of‑N, majority voting) by concentrating extra computation only on harder inputs.                                                      | **Low** — backbone is frozen; only a sub‑1M‑parameter controller is trained via GRPO on top of a one‑time pre‑computed steering vector — one of the cheapest methods in this table to train.                                                                                                      | Backbone‑training‑free, so it is easy to retrofit onto any existing model; tiny controller is cheap to train and deploy; explicitly adaptive to per‑instance difficulty rather than uniform.                                                                                                      | Relies on a separately pre‑computed "deliberative vs. direct" steering vector that must be re‑derived per backbone; gains are bounded by what a single steering direction can capture.                               |
| 8   | **R‑Capsule** — Shan et al., 2025, arXiv:2509.22131                                       | Compresses only the **high‑level strategic plan** of a reasoning trace into a small set of learned latent tokens (the "Reasoning Capsule") via an Information‑Bottleneck objective, while leaving low‑level execution steps explicit/lightweight — a hybrid of latent and explicit CoT.                                                                                                   | Significantly outperforms strong baselines on GSM8K and StrategyQA across model sizes; ablations show compressing _only the plan_ (not full execution) gives the best accuracy/efficiency trade‑off and produces interpretable capsules.                                            | **Medium** — trains a capsule encoder under a dual objective (low‑capacity IB bottleneck + auxiliary plan‑reconstruction loss); more than a single projection layer but far less than full‑model latent training.                                                                                 | Explicitly preserves interpretability for the execution steps while compressing only the "expensive" planning part; principled IB‑based minimality/sufficiency objective; works across model scales.                                                                                              | Hybrid design (latent plan + explicit execution) is more complex to implement than pure latent or pure explicit methods; benefit depends on tasks actually having a separable "plan vs. execute" structure.          |
| 9   | **SIM‑CoT** — Wei et al., 2025, arXiv:2509.20317 (ICLR 2026)                              | Identifies that scaling the number of implicit latent tokens causes **"latent collapse"** (representations become homogeneous / lose semantic diversity); fixes this with a plug‑and‑play **step‑level auxiliary decoder** that aligns each latent token to a specific reasoning step during training; the decoder is discarded at inference.                                             | On GPT‑2: **+2.1% over explicit CoT** with 2.3× greater token efficiency; **+8.2% over Coconut** and **+4.3% over CODI**. On LLaMA 1B/3B/8B: +1.5 to +9.0% gains, and — unlike prior methods — remains stable with 8–16 implicit tokens where Coconut/CODI collapse.                | **Medium** — adds a step‑level auxiliary decoder _only during training_ (discarded before deployment, so zero added inference cost); training is somewhat heavier but inference is unchanged from the base latent model.                                                                          | First method to _exceed_ explicit CoT accuracy while improving efficiency; diagnoses and directly fixes the latent‑collapse failure mode underlying many prior methods; no extra inference cost since the decoder is dropped after training; latents become human‑interpretable as a side effect. | Requires constructing step‑aligned supervision targets for the auxiliary decoder; benefit is most pronounced at higher latent‑token counts, which not all deployments need.                                          |
| 10  | **Pythia Arch / PonderLM‑2** — Zeng et al., 2025, arXiv:2509.23184 (ICML 2026)            | Pretrains a model to generate several "latent thought" hidden‑state vectors _before_ each real token prediction, feeding them back into itself — applying CoT‑style test‑time scaling to the **pretraining objective itself**, not just fine‑tuning.                                                                                                                                      | At equal inference FLOPs, a 1.4B model with one extra latent thought **outperforms a 2.8B vanilla model** trained on the same 300B‑token Pile data; a 1.26B variant matches Pythia‑2.8B with 55% fewer parameters and 62% less training data.                                       | **High** — this is a full pretraining‑scale method (300B tokens, multiple model sizes up to ~2.8B equivalent), the most compute‑intensive entry in this table by absolute training cost, even though it is more _parameter_‑efficient than naive scaling.                                         | Strong evidence that latent‑thought computation is a genuine alternative axis to parameter scaling; gains hold across a broad downstream‑task suite, not just math; data‑efficient relative to scaling parameters.                                                                                | Requires pretraining‑scale compute and data, putting it out of reach for most practitioners who only want to adapt an existing model; not a drop‑in fine‑tuning recipe.                                              |
| 11  | **CCoT** — Cheng & Van Durme, 2024, arXiv:2412.13171                                      | Introduces "contemplation tokens": a separate module generates **variable‑length, content‑rich continuous tokens** (compressed representations of an explicit reasoning chain) that the main LLaMA2‑7B‑chat model then reasons over.                                                                                                                                                      | On GSM8K, contemplation tokens at a 0.05 compression ratio still give a ~6‑point accuracy improvement over a no‑contemplation baseline, at the cost of only ~0.15s extra generation time; clearly outperforms fixed‑length Pause‑token baselines, which barely help.                | **Medium** — trains two modules (a hidden‑state generator and a decoder) on a 7B base model; no multi‑stage curriculum needed, but more total parameters/forward passes than single‑module methods.                                                                                               | Variable‑length compression (unlike fixed‑length Pause tokens) adapts to problem complexity; works on an off‑the‑shelf 7B chat model rather than only GPT‑2‑scale toy setups; modest latency overhead for real accuracy gains.                                                                    | Still requires language‑modeling‑objective fine‑tuning, which the SoftCoT authors later showed can cause catastrophic forgetting on instruction‑tuned LLMs; gains are GSM8K‑specific in the original paper.          |
| 12  | **HCoT** — Liu et al., 2024b, arXiv:2409.08561 _(= supplied paper #10)_                   | A separate **auxiliary CoT model** is trained (via a two‑stage procedure with a contrastive loss) to compress an entire explicit reasoning trace into a single compact special‑token representation, which is then fed into the main "Hidden CoT" model in place of the verbalized chain.                                                                                                 | Across mathematical reasoning, agent‑invocation, and QA benchmarks, reports **competitive‑or‑improved accuracy versus full explicit CoT** while substantially shortening output sequences and reducing latency/compute.                                                             | **Medium** — two‑stage training of a separate auxiliary model (contrastive alignment, then frozen‑auxiliary fine‑tuning of the main model); more moving parts than single‑model self‑distillation but no per‑query test‑time search.                                                              | Demonstrated across three distinct task families (math, agents, QA), suggesting good generality; auxiliary‑model design keeps the main LLM's training simpler; semantic‑alignment objective gives some interpretability of the compressed token.                                                  | Needs a separately trained and maintained auxiliary CoT model (extra system component); two‑stage training pipeline is more complex to reproduce than single‑stage distillation methods.                             |
| 13  | **LatentEvolve** — Zhang et al., 2025, arXiv:2509.24771                                   | Inspired by complementary‑learning‑systems theory: a "daytime" process rapidly retrieves and reuses past latent representations to guide current reasoning, while a "nighttime" process consolidates past latent optimizations — both operating purely at test time, with no parameter updates.                                                                                           | Surpasses prior test‑time‑scaling methods including **LatentSeek and TTRL by up to 13.3%** across eight benchmarks and five model backbones, with strong cross‑domain and cross‑backbone generalization; also yields shorter final decoding length than vanilla CoT.                | **Medium** — no fine‑tuning (purely test‑time), but it builds and queries a growing memory/skill repository of past latent trajectories across "daytime"/"nighttime" phases, adding non‑trivial inference‑time bookkeeping and retrieval cost over single‑shot test‑time methods.                 | Self‑evolving — performance improves over a session/deployment without any gradient updates; strong generalization across backbones and domains relative to single‑shot test‑time methods like LatentSeek.                                                                                        | Memory/retrieval system adds engineering complexity and state that must be managed across queries; benefits are inherently amortized (cold‑start has less to draw on than a "warmed‑up" deployment).                 |
| 14  | **LTA‑Thinker** — Wang et al., 2025, arXiv:2509.12875                                     | A lightweight assistant model (frozen backbone) generates Latent Thought Vectors from a **learnable‑prior architecture** designed to widen the variance of the latent‑thought distribution (motivated by SoftCoT++'s theory that higher variance better approximates the true latent distribution); trained with a combined Semantic‑Alignment (KL) + Reasoning‑Focus (contrastive) loss. | Reports **state‑of‑the‑art performance among latent‑thought baselines**, with a higher achievable performance ceiling and better scaling behavior than SoftCoT/SoftCoT++ as more latent thoughts are used.                                                                          | **Medium** — trains a small assistant model with two auxiliary losses (KL‑divergence semantic alignment + contrastive reasoning‑focus) while the main backbone stays frozen; heavier than plain SoftCoT but still far lighter than full‑model fine‑tuning.                                        | Directly engineers for a known theoretical bottleneck (insufficient latent‑distribution variance); dual‑loss design improves both relevance and focus of latent thoughts; backbone‑frozen design avoids forgetting.                                                                               | More hyperparameters (two loss weights, prior architecture) to tune than single‑loss assistant‑model methods; built explicitly on top of SoftCoT++'s theoretical assumptions, so it inherits that framework's scope. |
| 15  | **SoftCoT / SoftCoT++** — Xu et al., 2025, arXiv:2502.12134 (ACL 2025) / arXiv:2505.11484 | A small, **frozen** assistant LM speculatively generates instance‑specific "soft thought" tokens, which a trainable projection module maps into the frozen main LLM's embedding space — no fine‑tuning of the main LLM at all. SoftCoT++ extends this with multiple specialized initial tokens plus a contrastive objective to diversify thoughts, enabling parallel test‑time scaling.   | Outperforms Coconut on modern LLaMA/Qwen backbones (where Coconut actually underperforms zero‑shot CoT); SoftCoT++ further improves over SoftCoT via test‑time scaling and combines well with self‑consistency.                                                                     | **Low** — the large backbone LLM is never fine‑tuned; only a small assistant model and a lightweight projection layer are trained, avoiding both the cost and the catastrophic‑forgetting risk of full fine‑tuning — one of the cheapest ways to add latent reasoning to a strong existing model. | Parameter‑efficient and forgetting‑free since the main LLM is frozen; works well on modern instruction‑tuned LLMs (not just GPT‑2‑era toy models), where pure fine‑tuning methods like Coconut struggle; SoftCoT++ adds a genuine parallel test‑time‑scaling axis.                                | Performance is capped by what a frozen backbone can do when fed soft prompts (no weight adaptation); requires maintaining a separate assistant model alongside the main LLM.                                         |

| 16  | **Soft Thinking** — Zhang et al., 2025, arXiv:2505.15778 (NeurIPS 2025)                              | **Training‑free.** At each reasoning step, instead of sampling one discrete token, it forms a "concept token" as the probability‑weighted mixture of all vocabulary embeddings, letting the model implicitly explore multiple reasoning paths at once; a "Cold Stop" mechanism monitors entropy to halt before the soft state drifts too far out‑of‑distribution.                                                                                                     | Improves Pass@1 accuracy and token efficiency over standard/greedy CoT on math (MATH‑500, AIME24, GSM8K) and coding (HumanEval, MBPP, LiveCodeBench) benchmarks with QwQ‑32B and DeepSeek‑R1‑Distill models, while reducing generation length.                                               | **Low** — zero training cost; only requires patching the inference engine (implemented in SGLang) to accept and propagate weighted embeddings — essentially "free" to try on an existing model.                                                                                                                                                      | No training data or fine‑tuning needed at all; reuses the model's existing embedding matrix with no new parameters; easy to apply to any already‑deployed model.                                                                                                                                             | Follow‑up analysis ("LLMs are Single‑Threaded Reasoners") found that models largely collapse soft mixtures to their single top‑1 component in practice, undermining the claimed parallel‑path benefit unless extra randomness is injected; remains an out‑of‑distribution input for the base model, risking instability on long chains even with Cold Stop. |
| 17  | **HRPO** — Yue et al., 2025, arXiv:2505.18454 (NeurIPS 2025)                                         | **Hybrid Reasoning Policy Optimization**: a learnable gating mechanism blends discrete sampled‑token embeddings with continuous prior hidden states; training starts mostly token‑based and progressively incorporates more hidden‑state features, trained end‑to‑end via reinforcement learning — crucially, **without requiring any CoT traces** for supervision.                                                                                                   | Outperforms prior latent‑reasoning and SFT/RL baselines on both knowledge‑intensive and reasoning‑intensive benchmarks; models remain interpretable post‑training and exhibit emergent behaviors like cross‑lingual reasoning patterns and shorter completions.                              | **High** — full reinforcement‑learning (GRPO‑style) training of the base model's policy with a learnable gate; RL training is inherently more compute‑ and sample‑intensive, and less stable, than supervised distillation.                                                                                                                          | Does not need any CoT‑annotated training data, unlike most distillation‑based latent methods; retains stochasticity/interpretability since it mixes in real token samples rather than going fully continuous; gating lets it interpolate smoothly between discrete and latent reasoning.                     | RL training is expensive and can be unstable; gating/hybrid mechanism adds tuning surface (residual‑mix ratio, temperature); still requires careful curriculum (start mostly discrete, gradually add hidden features).                                                                                                                                      |
| 18  | **MoT‑G (Mixture of Tokens)** — Jain & Rappazzo, 2025, arXiv:2509.21482                              | A unified framework for **mixture‑of‑token generation inside RLVR** (reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards): instead of GRPO sampling one discrete token per step, it constructs explicit k‑token mixtures (e.g., top‑k + Dirichlet‑weighted) and trains with GRPO, preserving distributional information that standard discrete‑sampling RLVR discards.                                                                                                     | Reports gains on reasoning‑intensive benchmarks (evaluated on the Reasoning‑Gym suite) versus discrete‑token GRPO baselines, with accompanying mechanistic analysis of hidden‑state entropy/exploration showing richer exploration than discrete sampling.                                   | **High** — GRPO‑based RL training where rollouts are generated over token _mixtures_ rather than single tokens; RL rollout generation already multiplies compute versus SFT, and mixture‑based rollouts add further overhead per step.                                                                                                               | Directly targets a known limitation of discrete‑token RLVR (loss of distributional/uncertainty information); unifies several prior training‑free mixture‑embedding tricks into one RL‑compatible framework; provides interpretability analyses (entropy, exploration) alongside accuracy gains.              | RL‑based, so training cost and instability are higher than supervised alternatives; mixture construction (top‑k + Dirichlet weights) adds hyperparameters; concurrent with and conceptually overlapping "Soft Tokens, Hard Truths" (below), so relative advantages are still being worked out.                                                              |
| 19  | **CoLaR** — Tan et al., 2025, arXiv:2505.16552 (NeurIPS 2025)                                        | **Compressed Latent Reasoning**: in SFT, an auxiliary "next compressed‑embedding prediction" task merges _c_ consecutive token embeddings (c sampled randomly each step) and trains a latent head to predict the resulting distribution; an RL (GRPO) stage then exploits the latent head's non‑determinism to explore diverse paths and reward more compact ones. Users can simply _prompt_ the desired compression factor at inference for dynamic‑speed reasoning. | **14.1% higher accuracy** than latent‑based baselines at matched compression ratios; reduces reasoning‑chain length by **53.3%** with only 4.8% accuracy degradation vs. explicit CoT; with the RL stage, gains up to **+5.4% accuracy while cutting length by 82.8%** on harder math tasks. | **Medium‑High** — a two‑stage pipeline: SFT with an extra compressed‑embedding‑prediction objective, then a full GRPO reinforcement‑learning stage; more expensive than single‑stage SFT distillation but each stage is individually tractable.                                                                                                      | Inference‑time _controllable_ compression factor (no retraining needed to trade speed for accuracy); RL stage demonstrably buys both higher accuracy and shorter chains simultaneously, not a strict trade‑off; outperforms latent baselines at equal compression.                                           | Two‑stage training pipeline (SFT + RL) is more complex and costlier to reproduce than single‑stage methods; the RL stage carries the general instability risk associated with GRPO training.                                                                                                                                                                |
| 20  | **"Soft Tokens, Hard Truths"** — Butt, Kwiatkowski, Labiad, Kempe & Ollivier, 2025, arXiv:2509.19170 | First **scalable RL method to learn continuous CoT from scratch**, without distilling from any ground‑truth discrete CoT teacher: trains the model to explore a distribution of "soft" (continuous‑mixture) tokens during the CoT phase via a REINFORCE‑style objective, but **decodes hard (discrete) tokens at inference** — i.e., "train soft, infer hard."                                                                                                        | "Pass@1 parity, Pass@32 gains, and better robustness" vs. hard‑token CoT training, reported by the authors on Llama/Qwen models up to 8B on math/reasoning benchmarks; notably the _first_ work to make continuous‑CoT RL training scale without a discrete‑CoT teacher.                     | **High** — explicitly noted by the authors as solving a setting with "strong training difficulties" that prior work avoided (continuous‑token RL from scratch, no teacher distillation); this is among the most research‑/compute‑intensive entries in the table to get working at all, even though final inference uses ordinary discrete decoding. | Removes the dependency on a discrete‑CoT teacher entirely, unlike almost every other latent‑CoT method; "train soft, infer hard" means **zero extra inference cost or latency** versus a normal model, since deployment uses standard discrete decoding; improves sample diversity (Pass@32) and robustness. | Solving the RL‑without‑a‑teacher training problem required substantial methodological work, implying a non‑trivial training‑recipe/compute investment; Pass@1 parity (not improvement) suggests the main benefit is in diversity/robustness rather than raw single‑shot accuracy.                                                                           |

| 21  | **Token Assorted** — Su et al., 2025, arXiv:2502.03275 (ICML 2025)               | Trains a **VQ‑VAE** to quantize chunks of an explicit CoT trace (16 tokens per chunk, codebook size 1024) into discrete latent codes; the LLM is then trained on a randomized mix of latent codes and remaining text tokens, abstracting away the _initial_ reasoning steps while keeping later steps explicit.                                                                                                                                                           | Consistently outperforms baselines across Keys‑Finding Maze (from‑scratch training), and fine‑tuning settings on ProntoQA/ProsQA logical reasoning and GSM8K/MATH mathematical reasoning.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                          | **Medium‑High** — requires training a VQ‑VAE (100k steps) _before_ the main LLM fine‑tuning stage on the resulting hybrid latent/text sequences; two-stage pipeline with a non‑trivial codebook‑learning cost.                                                                   | Discrete codes are inherently more interpretable/auditable than continuous vectors (can be inspected like a small vocabulary); randomized‑replacement training gives fast adaptation to unseen latent tokens; works both from‑scratch and via fine‑tuning.                                  | VQ‑VAE introduces an extra training stage and hyperparameters (codebook size, chunk length, compression rate); discretization can lose information relative to continuous‑vector methods.                                                               |

## Analysis: Which Direction Is Most Promising for Efficient, High-Performance, Low-Cost Latent Token Compression?

No single method in the 38 surveyed above is a silver bullet, but a clear pattern emerges when the strongest results are placed side by side: the methods that buy the most accuracy and the most compression per dollar of training compute are not the ones that apply latent reasoning uniformly, but the ones that apply it _selectively_, and not the ones that supervise only the final answer, but the ones that supervise the latent process _densely_ while keeping that supervision free at inference time. Four converging threads in the evidence point toward a specific, synthesizable recipe rather than any one paper being "the answer."

The first thread is selective, instance-adaptive computation. Every method in this report that conditions _how much_ extra computation to spend on a per-token or per-instance basis outperforms its uniform counterpart, often dramatically. Think-at-Hard's central finding is that always iterating twice (its "AlwaysThink" baseline) actually _hurts_ accuracy relative to a single pass, because deeper iterations on already-correct, easy tokens introduce false corrections; routing only the hardest ~6% of tokens to a second pass instead delivers a 4–5.4 point accuracy gain at FLOPs that match the single-pass baseline. FR-Ponder gets similar leverage from a sub-million-parameter controller deciding when to "ponder" at all. SynAdapt's difficulty classifier and GainRouter's adaptive threshold do the same thing one level up, at the level of whole questions rather than tokens. CoLaR makes the compression factor itself a controllable, prompt-time knob rather than a fixed training-time choice. DeepConf applies the identical principle to a completely different axis (which parallel traces to keep), and gets an 84.7% token reduction precisely because it stops paying for traces that have already revealed themselves to be unpromising. The common mechanism across all of these is that uniform extra computation is wasted on the (typically large) fraction of a problem that the model already handles correctly, while a small, cheap controller that detects the hard fraction and routes compute there captures nearly all the benefit at a fraction of the cost.

The second thread is that the field's earlier "latent reasoning trails explicit CoT in accuracy" problem looks less like a fundamental property of compressing reasoning into vectors and more like a _supervision_ problem that has now been substantially solved. Coconut and the original CCoT both showed real accuracy losses relative to explicit CoT, and the survey itself lists this as an open challenge. But SIM-CoT's diagnosis is the pivotal result here: scaling up the number of latent tokens without step-level supervision causes "latent collapse," where the latents lose semantic diversity and converge toward encoding little more than bare numbers. Once SIM-CoT adds a step-level auxiliary decoder during training, the same underlying latent-token architecture not only closes the gap but exceeds explicit CoT by 2.1% with 2.3x better token efficiency, and the decoder is simply discarded before deployment, so this fix is free at inference time. MarCoS reaches a similar place from a different angle (a first-order Markov chain of continuous vectors, avoiding the single-static-latent bottleneck that earlier methods had) and beats token-based CoT by 4.7% on GSM8K with a 15.7x speedup. KaVa's step-wise KV-cache distillation and R-Capsule's information-bottleneck reconstruction loss are further instances of the same idea: give the latent process a dense training signal tied to intermediate structure, not just the final answer, and discard that signal before deployment. The practical implication is that "make latent compression match or beat explicit CoT" is now a solved sub-problem in isolation; the remaining work is in combining that solution with the selective-computation thread above.

The third thread concerns where the model-modification risk and cost actually sit. SoftCoT, LTA-Thinker, and FR-Ponder all keep the large backbone LLM completely frozen and train only a small assistant model, projection layer, or controller, which sidesteps the catastrophic-forgetting problem that the SoftCoT authors specifically documented when methods like Coconut and CCoT try to fine-tune an already strong, instruction-tuned LLM with a pure language-modeling objective. This "small auxiliary, frozen backbone" pattern is attractive less because it is theoretically elegant and more because it is the only pattern in this report that decouples the cost of adding latent compression from the cost of retraining the underlying model: an organization can retrofit it onto any existing strong LLM at LoRA-scale cost rather than re-running an expensive full fine-tune that risks degrading the model's other capabilities.

The fourth thread is that, once an SFT-trained base policy exists, a subsequent lightweight RL (typically GRPO) refinement pass reliably buys _both_ more accuracy and more compression at the same time rather than trading one for the other. CoLaR's RL stage delivers up to a 5.4 point accuracy gain while simultaneously cutting reasoning-chain length by a further 82.8%; the step-entropy paper's own ablation shows that SFT alone plateaus while adding GRPO is what unlocks the strongest matched-accuracy compression ratios; HRPO, MoT-G, and "Soft Tokens, Hard Truths" all report meaningful gains specifically attributable to moving from imitation-style training to outcome-driven RL. The cost of this is real (RL training is acknowledged across this report, and explicitly in the survey's own "Challenges" section, as less stable and more expensive than SFT), which is why the evidence points toward RL as a second-stage _polish_ applied after a stable SFT or distillation base is established, rather than as a starting point.

Put together, the direction this evidence points toward is not any single existing method but a composite recipe: a small, frozen-backbone controller that decides where and how much latent computation to allocate (the FR-Ponder/Think-at-Hard/SynAdapt pattern), operating over a continuous or lightly-discretized latent representation that receives dense, step-level supervision via an auxiliary decoder or reconstruction loss discarded before deployment (the SIM-CoT/MarCoS/R-Capsule pattern), trained first with cheap supervised distillation and only then refined with a GRPO-style RL pass to push the accuracy-compression frontier further (the CoLaR/step-entropy pattern). Several of 2025's strongest individual results are best read as partial instances of this composite recipe rather than as competing alternatives to it, which is why the field's trajectory looks convergent rather than scattered: SIM-CoT supplies the supervision piece, Think-at-Hard supplies the selective-allocation piece, CoLaR supplies the RL-polish piece, and SoftCoT-style frozen-backbone training supplies the low-deployment-risk piece, and no paper yet combines all four in one system.

One important caveat tempers this picture. The methods in Table 4 that stay in _explicit_, human-readable token space rather than moving to continuous latents (TokenSkip, CoT-Valve, the step-entropy method, and especially R1-Compress) consistently achieve smaller compression ratios (roughly 20-57%, versus 3x-15x+ for the strongest latent methods) but are markedly lower-risk to deploy: they require no architecture changes, work with existing inference servers and tooling, and crucially preserve full interpretability and auditability of the reasoning trace, which matters in regulated or safety-sensitive settings. R1-Compress's own analysis is a useful warning here: both C3oT and CoT-Valve, when compressing aggressively, were found to silently strip out the reflection and self-correction steps that long reasoning chains rely on for solving hard problems, an effect that would not necessarily show up as an alarming-looking number until the compressed model is stress-tested on genuinely difficult inputs. The survey itself names interpretability and unfaithful reasoning as a standing open challenge for latent CoT generally, and that concern applies with extra force to any system that pursues compression ratios beyond what current step-level-supervision and auxiliary-decoder techniques can keep faithful. So the practical recommendation splits in two: for a team that needs to ship efficiency gains today with minimal engineering and auditability risk, explicit-space chunk- or step-level compression (R1-Compress's chunk-search approach, or step-entropy pruning) is the safer choice despite its lower compression ceiling; for a team optimizing for the long-run efficiency frontier and willing to invest in the engineering of selective latent allocation plus step-level supervision plus an RL polish stage, the composite latent recipe above is where the strongest evidence in this report says the field is heading.
